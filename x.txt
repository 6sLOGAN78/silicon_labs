Malhotra, MD, DM (Critical Care Medicine / Senior ICU Consultant) (Hypothetical Panelist)1. Key Discussion Points & Clinical InsightsA. What Matters Most in Patient Handling (The Medical Perspective)Trend Tracking over Static Numbers: Dr. Malhotra emphasized that an isolated high heart rate or low BP reading is often a false alarm caused by patient movement or a loose sensor cable. What doctors actually look for are gradual mathematical trends (e.g., a slow, continuous drop in SpO2 combined with a steady rise in respiration rate over 45 minutes). Your system's focus on trend analysis is clinically valid.  The "Golden Window" for Sepsis: Sepsis is the leading cause of death in ICUs. The doctor stated that if an Edge AI tool can flag pre-septic shock markers even 30 to 60 minutes before visual symptoms appear, it allows doctors to start fluid resuscitation and empiric antibiotics early, reducing mortality rates drastically.B. Direct Feedback on ICUsentinel ArchitectureCombating Alarm Fatigue: The doctor heavily praised the "Patient Prioritization" objective. ICU nurses are suffering from severe alarm fatigue because current monitors beep constantly for minor issues. A system that ranks patients by an actual risk score helps staff know exactly whose room to run into first.  Approval of Local Edge Inference (EFR32MG26): Cloud-based medical tracking is risky because hospital Wi-Fi is notoriously unstable. Dr. Malhotra noted that executing TinyML locally on the patient node via the EFR32MG26 ensures that zero latency and zero connection drops happen during critical tracking.  C. Review of Fault Tolerance & Fallback ModesThe team presented the system’s fallback strategy (e.g., if the EFR32 edge node fails, the BGM220 data acquisition node bypasses it to send minimal vital packets directly to the central Raspberry Pi via BLE).  Doctor's Feedback: This feature is mandatory for medical certification. In an ICU, a software crash cannot mean patient tracking stops. The doctor advised making sure the central node sounds a specific "Hardware Malfunction Alarm" to let nurses know a node went offline.  2. Crucial Guidelines Formulated for the TeamBased on the consultation, the doctor defined what is critical to handle a patient safely. The team must ensure the following implementation rules:Prioritize False Negatives Over False Positives: In machine learning tuning, a false positive means a nurse checks a safe patient (annoying, but safe). A false negative means the AI missed a dying patient (fatal). The threshold tuning must strictly minimize false negatives.  Keep Hardware Non-Invasive: Keep data collection limited to simple non-invasive sensors (ECG electrodes, BP cuff, and Pulse Oximeter) to avoid complicating ICU bedside space.  3.
